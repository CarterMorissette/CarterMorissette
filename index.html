Predicting NBA win percentage from box-score statistics and salary-cap efficiency

A replication and extension of a 2015–16 regression paper, rebuilt in R and pushed forward a decade. The original authors validated on a single season with no out-of-sample test — a real overfitting risk with only thirty teams and an interaction term. I refit the model on 2024–25 data to see whether the relationships held up, then added share of salary cap used as a predictor to ask a question the original never did: does spending buy wins?

The first model I fit had eleven predictors and an adjusted R² of 95.05%. Two of them — total rebounds and turnovers forced — carried p-values above 0.14 and VIFs above 5, meaning they were both statistically idle and tangled up with predictors that weren't.

Dropping them raised adjusted R² to 95.33% while plain R² fell slightly. That's the trade working exactly as it should: the model lost a little in-sample fit and gained explanatory honesty. Miami's standardized residual fell from 2.13 to 1.90 and stopped being an outlier — not because the Heat changed, but because the model stopped straining to explain them with variables that had no business in it.

A higher R² is almost always available. It's rarely the thing worth having.

On the 2024–25 NBA salary-cap model · July 2026

Spending doesn't buy wins. Efficiency does.

When I added share of salary cap used to a model that already knew how teams played, it stopped mattering. p = 0.384. That number is the whole argument.

The setup was simple. I had a regression predicting team win percentage from box-score statistics — three-point percentage, points, defensive rebounds, assists, steals, turnovers, opponent field goal percentage, opponent points. It worked well: adjusted R² of 95.48% on the 2024–25 season. Then I added one more predictor, the percentage of the salary cap a team had spent, and asked whether money explained anything the on-court numbers didn't.

It didn't. The F-statistic on salary cap share came back at 0.79, with a p-value of 0.384. Meanwhile three-point percentage posted an F of 286.45. Shooting, scoring, turnovers, and defense were all strongly tied to winning. The size of the payroll, once those were in the model, barely moved the needle.

The naive reading is that spending is irrelevant. That's wrong, and it's worth being careful here. Cap share doesn't predict wins conditional on performance — but performance is precisely what payroll is supposed to buy. The regression isn't telling us money doesn't matter. It's telling us money is not the mechanism. Spend and production are related, but the relationship is mediated entirely by whether the spending actually converts into shooting, defense, and ball security. When you control for the conversion, the spend has nothing left to say.

Which reframes the front-office question. Not how much cap space should we use, but how many points of three-point percentage does this contract buy. The model gives no credit for approaching the cap. It gives credit for what shows up in the box score. Two teams can spend identically and land two standard deviations apart in win percentage, and the regression will explain the gap without ever looking at their payrolls.

This is the Moneyball observation, arriving from a different direction. Beane's insight was that the market mispriced on-base percentage. The version here is narrower and less romantic: the market prices players, but wins are produced by production, and the gap between those two things is where the whole competitive advantage lives. Every dollar of that gap is a dollar of wins your opponent paid for and didn't get.

Two teams show what this looks like in practice. The Lakers' standardized residual was +2.09 — they won substantially more than their statistical profile predicted, which is either coaching, health, chemistry, or luck, and the model cannot tell you which. Philadelphia sat at −2.06 in the other direction. Neither residual has anything to do with what those franchises spent. Both are the model admitting there's something in a basketball season it doesn't have a column for.

That's the honest place to end. A regression with an adjusted R² of 95.45% explains almost everything and still cannot explain the Lakers. The residuals are where the interesting questions start, not where the analysis stops.
